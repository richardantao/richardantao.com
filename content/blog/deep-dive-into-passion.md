The word ‘*passion*’ is one that gets thrown around a lot.

- *“I’m passionate about my job”.*
- *“I’m passionate about reading”.*
- *“We’re looking for someone passionate about [X industry]”.*
- *“The passion just isn’t there anymore”.*

We have all heard these phrases a thousand times, and at a first glance, there’s nothing wrong with them. But have we ever stopped and wondered what the word really means? What are people intending to communicate when they talk about passion, and how does passion differ from concepts such as interest, desire, or love?

## Origins

I always find looking at the etymology of words very interesting, because the evolution of the word often highlights the cultural changes that occurred alongside the word across time. For *passion*, it’s origins take us back over 2,000 years, to the death of Jesus Christ. In particular, the *Passion of Christ*.

The *Passion of Christ* refers to the suffering, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ, specifically focusing on the events during his final days.

From this, we can derive the modern English word of ‘*passion’,* from the Latin word ‘*pati’*, which literally means *to suffer*.

Putting aside the elements of religion, faith, and dogma, I can’t help but wonder how on earth we derived the modern applications of the word *passion* from ultimate suffering?

## Modern Usage

Modern definitions for *passion* will often resort to heightened emotions and states, such as:

- strong and barely controllable emotion
- a state or outburst of emotion
- intense sexual love
- a thing arousing enthusiasm
